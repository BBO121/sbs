War Burned More Than Just Oil: The Middle East, Where Even Air, Water, and the Sea Are Collapsing

[작은 설명 small caption]
From Gaza to Tehran - Life Conditions Destroyed by War

[작성자 부분]
Text: Greenpeace Seoul Office

[부제 - 01]
On March 8, black rain fell in Tehran.

    [문단 - 01]
    On March 8, Israeli forces launched airstrikes on four oil storage facilities and one oil pipeline in the Tehran area of ​​Iran. The Iranian capital, home to approximately 9 million people, was engulfed in black smoke, and rainwater mixed with heavy metals, sulfur oxides, and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) resulting from oil combustion poured down. The World Health Organization (WHO) officially warned that this black acid rain was dangerous to Iranian citizens. The Iranian Red Cross advised residents to take shelter indoors.

    [문단 - 02]
    More than 50 days have passed since the war began on February 28. Environmental damage is accumulating in various locations, including Iran, Lebanon, and the Gaza Strip. The UK-based Conflict Environment Watch (CEOBS) reported that by early March, it had recorded over 300 incidents of environmental damage across Iran, Israel, and 12 Gulf countries, and that such damage continues to accumulate. Bloomberg tallied that dozens of oil refineries, oil fields, gas processing facilities, and ports across the Middle East have been struck by missiles and drones. The United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) and the World Health Organization (WHO) have issued statements and warnings, respectively, and a petition regarding ecocide has been submitted to the UN Secretary-General. The genocide and environmental destruction occurring in the Gaza Strip, the U.S.-Israel war against Iran, and Israel's bombing of Lebanon are simultaneously destroying ecosystems and the foundations of civilian life across the entire Middle East.

[부제 - 02]
Two and a half years, Gaza's quiet eco-slaughter

    [문단 - 01]
    Long before the outbreak of the U.S.-Israel war against Iran, another massacre had been systematically destroying the environment. In Gaza, Israeli military operations have been ongoing for two and a half years since October 2023. According to an official report released by the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) on September 23, 2025 , 97% of tree crops, 95% of shrublands, and 82% of annual crops in Gaza have been destroyed since 2023. Large-scale food production has become impossible.

    [문단 - 02]
    The same applies to water. The collapse of the sewage treatment system, the destruction of the pipeline network, and the temporary use of septic tanks have contaminated the groundwater that most Gaza residents rely on. While it is highly likely that the coast and ocean are also contaminated, testing is currently impossible. As a result, waterborne diseases have skyrocketed.

    [문단 - 03]
    Buildings and infrastructure have collapsed. 78% of Gaza's approximately 250,000 buildings were damaged or destroyed. The resulting debris amounts to 61 million tons. Of this, 15% is at risk of contamination with asbestos, industrial waste, and heavy metals. It is estimated that it will take 15 years just to clear this debris.

    [문단 - 04]
    According to an analysis by Greenpeace Middle East and North Africa (MENA), more than 500,000 tons of carbon dioxide were emitted in Gaza during the first 120 days of the war alone. This is greater than the combined annual emissions of over 100 climate-vulnerable countries. A research team from Queen Mary University and Lancaster University estimates the long-term carbon cost, including the reconstruction process, at 31 million tons. A network of Palestinian environmental NGOs has characterized what is happening in Gaza as "clear ecocide." What is currently taking place in Tehran and Lebanon is an extension of what has been happening in Gaza for the past two and a half years.

[부제 - 03]
In a place where people live

    [문단 - 01]
    The same research team that analyzed the environmental impact of the war in Gaza estimated that greenhouse gases emitted during the first 14 days of the war with Iran amounted to over 5 million tons (in CO₂ equivalent). This figure exceeds Iceland's annual carbon emissions. Of this, 2.4 million tons came from the destruction of homes and infrastructure. According to the Iranian Red Cross, approximately 20,000 civilian buildings were damaged. This figure is expected to increase rapidly as the war continues.

    [문단 - 02]
    The citizens of Tehran are not merely breathing in smoke from oil fires. They are being exposed to concrete dust, asbestos, and chemicals emanating from buildings destroyed by bombing. Because Tehran is situated in a basin surrounded by mountains, it is difficult for pollutants to escape. Even after the bombing has ended, citizens continue to be exposed to hazardous substances. This is because the same materials are released back into the air during the reconstruction process, as debris is demolished and buildings are rebuilt.

    [문단 - 03]
    It is not just air. Damage to water infrastructure continues across the Middle East. Desalination facilities in Iran were damaged by U.S. airstrikes. Desalination facilities in Bahrain were damaged by Iranian drone attacks. Fuel tanks at Kuwait International Airport were also struck by Iranian drones. Saudi Arabia's Ras Tanura oil refinery, the UAE's Ruwais oil refinery, and the Port of Fujairah were also attacked. Although the perpetrators of the attacks differ, the destruction is the same: facilities essential for people to drink water, move around, and live. Mohamed Mahmoud, Head of Middle East Climate and Water Policy at the United Nations Institute of Water, Environment and Health, stated, "These are people who struggle to access clean drinking water even in times of peace," adding that "the damage to water infrastructure lasts for a long time."

    [문단 - 04]
    The situation in Lebanon is also rapidly deteriorating. Israel resumed airstrikes on March 2, destroying at least six bridges over the Litani River and bombing water facilities and solar power plants in southern Lebanon. Human Rights Watch reported the use of white phosphorus bombs in residential areas. White phosphorus can burn to the bone upon contact with skin and contaminates soil and water. Greenpeace Middle East and North Africa (MENA) stated that the patterns of destruction seen in Gaza—attacks on medical facilities, destruction of civilian infrastructure, and widespread environmental damage—are being repeated in Lebanon, and that these scorched-earth tactics could constitute war crimes.

[부제 - 04]
Wars the Sea Remembers

    [문단 - 01]
    In late March, about 20 ships were attacked in the Persian Gulf. Fuel and heavy oil leaked from sunken warships, and oil also spilled from some oil tankers. The area contains coral reefs, seagrass habitats, and nesting grounds for dugongs and sea turtles.

    [문단 - 02]
    The Persian Gulf is one of the fastest-growing sea regions on Earth. Coral reefs and seagrass communities are already under pressure due to climate change, compounded by oil pollution caused by the war. Greenpeace Germany analyzed ship movement data and satellite imagery to simulate how oil would spread in the event of an attack, noting that over 85 tankers trapped in the Persian Gulf are carrying a total of 21 billion liters of crude oil. Nina Noelle of Greenpeace Germany warned, "Just a single oil spill in the Persian Gulf could irreparably damage this fragile marine ecosystem." In this shallow and enclosed area, pollutants do not dilute as quickly as they do in the open sea. Heavy metals and hazardous substances that settle in seabed sediments remain there for a long time.

    [문단 - 03]
    This sea already carries the memory of war. During the Iran-Iraq War in the 1980s, oil tankers and platforms were extensively attacked. In the 1991 Gulf War, Iraqi forces set fire to approximately 700 oil wells in Kuwait, which burned for months. At that time, Greenpeace conducted on-site investigations into oil contamination off the Iranian coast. Now, 35 years later, a third war and a third destruction are continuing in the same sea.

[부제 - 05]
Invisible threat

    [문단 - 01]
    Since the outbreak of the war in Iran, the Bushehr nuclear power plant, Iran's only operating nuclear facility, has been attacked four times. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi criticized the international community's "indifference" to nuclear safety. While the UN nuclear monitoring agency (IAEA) urged all parties to exercise restraint, access for on-site inspections of the bombed Iranian nuclear facilities remains blocked. The Prime Minister of Qatar released simulation results regarding the attack on the Bushehr plant, stating that "the sea will be completely contaminated, and the water will run out within three days."

    [문단 - 02]
    Jan Vande Putte, a nuclear and radiation expert at Greenpeace International, warned that an attack on the power grid could cause a chain reaction of failures in the Bushehr nuclear power plant's cooling system, escalating into a cross-border nuclear crisis. Since the Bushehr plant is located on the coast of the Persian Gulf, marine pollution in the event of an accident would spread to the entire Gulf region.

    [문단 - 03]
    Nuclear power plants are not the only invisible threat. A significant number of bombed Iranian military facilities are buried deep underground or located near residential areas. It is not fully understood what substances are seeping into the soil and groundwater.

[부제 - 06]
Even after the war ended

    [문단 - 01]
    Regarding this war, Kaveh Madani, Director of the Institute of Water, Environment and Health at the United Nations University, stated, "As someone who has fought for environmental sustainability, this is a step backward by years." Doug Weir, Director of CEOBS, points out, "Because humanitarian needs come first, environmental damage is not properly addressed even after most conflicts." Contaminated soil, seabeds settled with heavy metals, and facilities potentially contaminated by radioactivity—these are the things that remain even after the war ends.

    [문단 - 02]
    The list of things destroyed by war is long. The farmlands and drinking water of Gaza, the air of Tehran, the seas of the Persian Gulf, the drinking water of Bahrain and Iran, the water pipes and bridges of Lebanon, and the safety around the Bushehr nuclear power plant. This is not a list of military targets. It is a list of the conditions under which people breathe, drink, and live.

    [문단 - 03]
    Environmental damage caused by war is not a collateral loss; it is violence in itself. However, current international law offers virtually no effective means to sanction environmental destruction resulting from war. It is time to hold the perpetrators of this violence accountable. As long as immunity persists, the same thing will happen again.

    [문단 - 04]
    Greenpeace demands the following: an unconditional and permanent ceasefire in all parts of the Middle East embroiled in this war, including Gaza and Lebanon; a halt to all state actions targeting civilians; an end to Israel's illegal occupation of Palestine; a comprehensive arms embargo and sanctions against those responsible for violating international law; unhindered access to relief by the UN and humanitarian organizations; and the safe passage of the global Sumud Flotilla currently crossing the Mediterranean.

    [문단 - 05]
    Furthermore, it demands that we confront the vulnerability of fossil fuel dependence revealed by this war and transform our fundamental structure into a renewable energy-centered society that is not swayed by the energy of war. The path to breaking free from the war economy is the path to a peace economy.
